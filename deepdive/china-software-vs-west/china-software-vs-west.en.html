TL;DR / 30-Second Core

Anthropic's business model is a product of the US cloud-native software market — cloud delivery, standard agents, and a PE distribution network form a unified triad. Entering China means all three layers need to be completely redesigned: the deployment layer becomes private, the product layer becomes deeply customized, and the distribution layer becomes a state-owned enterprise trust chain. It's not impossible, but the transformation cost and timeline are severely underestimated.

Counter-consensus insight

The primary competitor for a Chinese version of Anthropic is not DeepSeek or Zhipu, but Yonyou and Kingdee. The data is in the ERP, the trust is in the ERP, and the decision budget is in the ERP. Before entering large enterprise clients, LLM companies must first answer a prerequisite question: embed into the ERP, or replace the ERP from the outside? The two paths have completely different gross margins, sales motions, and capital structures — there is no middle ground.

§ 01 / BASELINE

Three Kingdoms:
Software Industry Baselines

To evaluate the portability of the Anthropic model, we must first establish the software industry baselines of three markets — not market size, but structure: business models, deployment preferences, and revenue composition. The gaps between the three markets are larger than most people realize.

🇺🇸 US
Market size:Enterprise software ~$280 billion (2024E, incl. SaaS)
SaaS penetration:~40%, cloud-native is the default for new deployments
Revenue structure:Subscription ~65%, professional services ~15%, maintenance & support ~10%, perpetual license ~10%
Customization rate:Large enterprise implementations ~25% custom, 75% standard configuration
Procurement cycle:3–6 months (CTO/CIO-led, high technical evaluation weight)
Gov/SOE share:~15–20% of software spending
Distribution ecosystem:GSI (Accenture/Deloitte) + PE portfolio networks + cloud marketplaces
🇪🇺 EUROPE
Market size:Enterprise software ~$90 billion (2024E)
SaaS penetration:~25–28%, Germany/France lean conservative (German On-Prem preference ~40%)
Revenue structure:Subscription ~40%, professional services/custom ~25%, maintenance & support ~20%, perpetual license ~15%
Customization rate:~35%, German Mittelstand especially high (up to 50%)
Procurement cycle:6–9 months (compliance officer involvement, GDPR review is mandatory)
Gov/SOE share:~25–30%
Distribution ecosystem:GSI (Capgemini/Infosys/SAP SI) + national clouds (Gaia-X initiative)
🇨🇳 CHINA
Market size:Enterprise software ~¥340 billion (2024E, ~$47 billion)
SaaS penetration:~10–15%, large enterprises universally require private or hybrid deployment
Revenue structure:SaaS ~10%, custom implementation ~35%, maintenance & support ~15%, perpetual license ~40%
Customization rate:~70%, SOE/central SOE projects can reach 80%+
Procurement cycle:9–18 months (multi-layer committees + compliance + domestic certification)
Gov/SOE share:~40–45%, the absolute mainstay of China's software revenue
Distribution ecosystem:State-owned SIs + ERP vendor channels (Yonyou/Kingdee) + cloud vendors, no independent PE network
Key Comparison Conclusion

Europe is the closest reference point to China among the three: data sovereignty constraints (GDPR ≈ Data Security Law + PIPL), higher government procurement share, and stronger On-Prem preference — these three points share structural similarities with China. But China adds three unique layers on top: LLM filing requirements, Xinchuang domestic substitution mandates, and the political review chain in SOE procurement. European adaptation experience has reference value but cannot be directly copied.

§ 02 / DIFFERENCES

Six Structural Differences
Deconstructed Layer by Layer

Difference 01 / Deployment Model ← The First Wall
🇺🇸 US

New deployments default to cloud; AWS/Azure hosting is the standard path. Private deployment is the exception, seen only in government classified or special industries (defense/nuclear)

🇩🇪 Germany (representing Europe)

Mittelstand ~40% still prefer On-Prem; GDPR requires data to stay within specific jurisdictions, driving "sovereign cloud" (Deutsche Telekom/OVHcloud) demand

🇨🇳 China

Private deployment is the default, not the exception. Central SOEs/state-owned banks/critical infrastructure data cannot leave local data centers — this is a hard compliance requirement, not a negotiating chip. 64% of large enterprise AI procurement requires private deployment

→ Anthropic's cloud API delivery model fails directly in China's large enterprise market.A privately deployable version that runs independently in customer data centers must be provided, which means: GPU procurement/leasing costs shift from cloud to vendor, fundamentally altering the gross margin structure.

Difference 02 / Customization Culture: Standard Product vs Tailor-Made
US
25% custom

"Fit to standard" culture: enterprises willing to adapt internal processes for standard products. Salesforce and Workday's success is built on the philosophy of "configuration, not development"

Europe
35% custom

German SMEs especially lean toward customization: the "Made in Germany" spirit extends to software, with lower willingness to standardize processes; SAP's rise itself served this need

China
70% custom

SOE/central SOE projects can reach 80%+.Historically, due to rampant ERP piracy, Yonyou/Kingdee built competitive moats through extensive customization services; enterprise clients thus developed the mindset that "software is a custom product"

Anthropic model's fatal assumption:Pre-built agents (e.g., 10 financial agents) cover 80% of scenarios. This is reasonable in the US — in China, every client's report formats, approval workflows, and data fields are different. A standard product claiming to "cover 80%" is eliminated before entering the negotiation room. The China version of agents must embed a customization framework, not pre-built fixed templates.
Difference 03 / Business Model: Subscription vs License + Implementation
US (Classic SaaS-era path)

Subscription-first:Salesforce seat fee $25–300/month, Anthropic Claude Enterprise API by token consumption + fixed seat fee, revenue is predictable and scalable

Light implementation:GSIs charge implementation fees; product vendors don't participate in implementation, gross margin structure is clean

Upgrade path:Automatic version updates, invisible to customers, marginal cost approaches zero

China (Still migrating from license to subscription)

Perpetual license dominant:Enterprises accustomed to one-time buyout; Yonyou FY2024 revenue of ¥9.15 billion with subscription only ~20%, the rest from license fees + implementation fees

Implementation revenue is a profit center:Software license : implementation fee = 1:3 to 1:5 (vs ~1:1 in the US); implementation teams are the core competitive capability

Upgrade resistance:Extensive customization code means version upgrades require re-implementation; customers tend not to upgrade — generating massive "implementation debt"

→ Pure subscription / pure API consumption pricing logic requires a hybrid model in China's large enterprise market: subscription base + implementation services + custom development, which means building implementation delivery capability

Difference 04 / Distribution System ← The Most Severely Underestimated Difference

Anthropic's PE JV model works in the US because Blackstone/KKR etc. hold 2,000+ portfolio companies; a JV partnership simultaneously opens procurement channels to all of them. This structure does not exist in China.

US: PE Networks

Blackstone portfolio companies 2,700+; one JV agreement is equivalent to issuing entry tickets to 2,700 enterprises. GPM unaffected, distribution marginal cost extremely low

Europe: GSI-led

Capgemini/Sopra Steria are primary distribution channels; Europe lacks equivalent PE networks, but GSI penetration is high, and covering large enterprises through GSI partnerships is relatively feasible

China: SOE Trust Chain

No independent PE network; the core of distribution is the SOE trust chain: ICBC/China Mobile/State Grid procurement → other central SOEs reference → leading private enterprises follow. Building the trust chain takes 2–4 years

The correct approach for China JV partners:Not US-style PE (Chinese PE/VC don't hold operating enterprises and can't "bundle" procurement channels), but rather: ① Central SOE subsidiary industry funds (providing compliance credit + procurement entry) + ② Top cloud vendors (providing technical pipeline + compute) + ③ ERP vendor strategic partnerships (providing existing customer data access). All three are indispensable.
Difference 05 / Procurement Decision Chain: Technical Evaluation vs Political Approval
US + Europe (Technology-driven)

CTO/CIO-led, technical POC is standard process; Europe adds Data Protection Officer (DPO) and legal review, but the overall path is clear. Dimon's endorsement = JPMorgan legal compliance sign-off = industry entry ticket

Decision layers: 2–3 · Cycle: 3–9 months
China (Multi-committee + Compliance)

Typical large SOE procurement path: Technical department research → IT committee review → Information security compliance audit (MLPS, data security) → Procurement committee → (Party committee) deliberation → Leadership approval. Each step can become a bottleneck

Decision layers: 5–7 · Cycle: 9–18 months

→ China's version of "Dimon endorsement" = ICBC president's public backing; but far harder to obtain than in the US, and SOEs typically don't simultaneously procure competing products — once won, the lock-in effect is stronger

Difference 06 / Regulatory Architecture: Europe Is Already the Heaviest, China Adds Three More Layers
US
  • · FedRAMP (government cloud certification)
  • · SOC 2 Type II
  • · Industry regulation (HIPAA/PCI-DSS)
  • · No unified AI regulation (federal level still fragmented)
Europe
  • · GDPR (data localization + user rights)
  • · NIS2 (critical infrastructure cybersecurity)
  • · EU AI Act (additional obligations for high-risk AI)
  • · Cross-border data assessments (SCCs/BCRs)
China (Europe + 3 additional layers)
  • · Data Security Law + PIPL (data cannot leave borders)
  • · MLPS 2.0 / Level 3 Protection (mandatory certification)
  • · LLM service filing (generative AI services must be registered)
  • · Xinchuang domestic substitution (SOEs/government must use domestic base software)
  • · Critical Information Infrastructure Operator (CIIO) special requirements
Xinchuang's far-reaching impact:SOE/government projects require AI systems to run on domestic CPUs (FeiTeng/Kunpeng) + domestic OS (Kylin/UOS), meaning Anthropic not only needs private deployment but also adaptation for non-x86 architectures — extremely high technical cost, and impossible to circumvent.

§ 03 / ANATOMY

Anthropic
Model Anatomy

Anthropic's enterprise AI business model consists of three interdependent layers. Understanding why each layer works in the US is essential to precisely determining which layer will fail first in China.

Layer 1 / Model Capability Layer API delivery · Consumption billing · Cloud-hosted
Why it works in the US

Enterprises can directly access Claude via the Anthropic API with no on-premises deployment. Cloud hosting means Anthropic's compute costs are amortized across Google/Amazon infrastructure, GPM ~70%+. Token billing enables "pay for what you use," lowering the procurement barrier

Failure point in China

Cloud API is ineffective for the 64% of large enterprises requiring private deployment. Even accepting a hybrid model, LLM service filing requires domestic services to use registered models. Foreign models face compliance barriers providing services within China; Anthropic itself cannot directly enter core scenarios in API form

Layer 2 / Vertical Agent Layer Pre-built · Standardized · Finance/Legal/HR scenarios
Why it works in the US

US financial industry report formats (SEC 10-K/10-Q) and compliance processes (SOX/Dodd-Frank) are highly standardized. 10 financial agents can cover 80% of common scenarios at JPMorgan with no customization. GSIs handle the remaining 20% adaptation

Failure point in China

China's financial industry report formats are fragmented (A-share/Hong Kong/CBRC/CSRC/PBOC formats are mutually inconsistent), OA approval workflows are highly individualized, and supply chain data standards lack unification. Standard agents' "out-of-the-box" promise fails before entering the meeting room — every enterprise will say "our situation is somewhat unique"

Layer 3 / Distribution Trust Layer ← Hardest to Transform PE JV · Trust endorsement · Enterprise penetration
Why it works in the US

A JV agreement with Blackstone etc. simultaneously opens procurement channels to 2,700+ portfolio companies. Dimon's public endorsement = JPMorgan legal compliance has reviewed = other financial industry CTOs can reference this precedent to reduce their own decision risk

Failure point in China

Chinese PE/VC don't hold operating enterprises' procurement rights and can't "bundle" channels. Trust signals come from different sources: SOE CEO endorsement > private CEO endorsement. Building an SOE trust chain requires not JV equity structures, but real deployment cases + government procurement records — which take 2–4 years to accumulate

→ Layer 3 is the hardest to transform among the three. Layer 1 and Layer 2 can be solved through technical investment; Layer 3 requires time and political capital — neither of which can be accelerated by money.
