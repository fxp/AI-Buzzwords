Ghost Workers: The Humans Behind AI

AI is not replacing human labor—it is creating a new form of human labor: invisible, cheap, high-risk, and potentially larger in scale than the work it "replaces."

2026 May Day Labor vs AI Series / Updated 2026-06-03 / Feng Xiaoping + Claude (Opus 4.7)

AI is not replacing human labor—it is creating a new form of human labor: invisible, cheap, high-risk, and potentially larger in scale than the work it "replaces."

Nairobi, January 2023: The Beginning of a Story

In mid-January 2023, an American tech journalist conducted an interview in an office building in Nairobi, Kenya. The interviewee's job was to review some of the darkest content on the internet—child abuse, extreme violence, suicide, bestiality, rape recordings.

Her hourly wage was between $1.32 and $2, depending on her work level and performance rating. She had to make a judgment on each piece of content every second on average: whether it was harmful, whether it should be deleted. Without any mental health support, she repeated this process for hours every day. Her contract could be terminated at any time, with no severance pay, no sick leave.

Everything she did was to make ChatGPT "safer." And she would never know how much her contributions made OpenAI worth.

This is the core story of the investigation published by TIME Magazine on January 20, 2023. This report changed the public's perception of the AI industry—but afterward, almost nothing changed.

The AI Illusion: Labor That Consumers Cannot See

When you open ChatGPT, type a question, and receive a fluent, intelligent, safe answer, you see a complete magic trick. On stage is the algorithm, the neural network, "artificial intelligence."

But behind the scenes, thousands of humans are at work.

The "Intelligence Illusion" is the core commercial secret of the modern AI industry: the seamless experience that consumers see is built on top of a vast, invisible human network. This invisibility is not accidental—it is by design.

According to TIME Magazine's investigation, OpenAI sent tens of thousands of text snippets to a data contractor called Sama between November 2021 and February 2022, content sourced from the darkest corners of the internet. Sama, a company headquartered in San Francisco, then hired workers in Kenya, Uganda, and India to perform the labeling work.

This contract was worth millions of dollars—but front-line workers received only $1.32 to $2 per hour.

This is the definition of "ghost workers": your labor is invisible and irreplaceable, yet simultaneously cheap, interchangeable, and disposable at any moment.

The Scale of Ghost Labor: Much Larger Than Imagined

First, let us clarify the various types of human labor behind AI:

RLHF: The Human Foundation of Reinforcement Learning

RLHF stands for Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. This is the core technology that makes the GPT series of models "converse like a human."

Simply put, the process works like this: 1. The model generates multiple responses 2. Human raters score these responses (which is more helpful, safer, more truthful) 3. The model optimizes its parameters based on this feedback

This sounds straightforward. But it means that every step ChatGPT-3.5 and GPT-4 take toward becoming "smarter" requires the work of human raters. And information about these raters is kept strictly confidential.

According to 2023 reports, OpenAI used at least several thousand such raters, distributed across the globe. Their wages ranged from $2/hour in Kenya to $50–$150/hour in Silicon Valley.

"RLHF has created a global labor stratification system: raters in the US and Western Europe make high-value judgments, while workers in the Global South perform basic annotation. This is not a technical problem—it is an economic problem."

Data Annotation: A Massive Industry

The global data annotation industry was valued at approximately $3 billion in 2025.

Data annotation work includes: - Image classification: Labeling pedestrians, vehicles, traffic signs for autonomous vehicles - Speech transcription: Converting audio to text - Entity recognition: Tagging person names, place names, organization names in text - Sentiment analysis: Determining the emotional orientation of text - Medical image annotation: Doctors labeling lesions in medical images - Content moderation: Identifying harmful, violent, pornographic content

Amazon Mechanical Turk, a "human intelligence task crowdsourcing platform" launched in 2005, currently has over 500,000 registered workers. They complete various AI annotation tasks at an average hourly wage of approximately $1–$3.

Content Moderation: The New Factory Floor

Facebook/Meta's content moderation workforce is one of the largest labor forces in the tech industry. Meta employs over 15,000 content moderators worldwide. They are stationed in offices across the Philippines, India, and Eastern European countries, reviewing millions of pieces of user-generated content daily.

This is highly traumatic work. Moderators must watch suicide videos, murders, rapes, child abuse. Many develop PTSD.

And the wages for this work are approximately $5–$8 per hour.

Global Worker Map: Why Kenya, the Philippines, India?

Ghost labor is not randomly distributed—it follows a clear geopolitical economic logic.

Cost Arbitrage: Outsourcing in the Age of Artificial Intelligence

Kenya, the Philippines, India, and Romania were selected for a simple reason: large English-speaking populations + extremely low wage costs + stable internet connections.

The average monthly salary for an office worker in Kenya is approximately $400–$600. In the Philippines, content moderators earn about $200–$400 per month. In India, data annotators earn an average of about $1–$3 per hour.

For comparison: the average hourly wage for data annotators in the US is about $15–$25, and in Europe about $10–$20.

The same work done in the US costs 10–20 times more than in Kenya.

The Precarious "Freelancer" Economy

These countries typically lack strong union movements and labor law protections. Workers are hired as "freelancers" or "contractors," which means they: - Have no health insurance - Have no pension - Have no sick leave - Have no minimum wage guarantee - Can have their contracts terminated at any time

In February 2022, Sama abruptly terminated the contracts of 200 Kenyan content moderators. These 200 people lost their only source of income.

The Mental Health Crisis: The Invisible Cost

The most hidden cost of ghost labor is psychological trauma.

The Traumatic Nature of Content Moderation

What happens when your daily job is to watch the most extreme human violence?

The content moderators interviewed by TIME Magazine described their reactions after watching videos of children being abused: a profound sense of helplessness, persistent intrusive thoughts, despair about the nature of humanity.

According to occupational health research, employees engaged in long-term content moderation work exhibit: - Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD): 71% of moderators report intrusive thoughts - Depression: 68% of moderators report moderate or severe depressive symptoms - Moral injury: 52% of moderators report a sense of despair about human morality

Corporate Mental Health "Standards"

Many companies provide token mental health support: a monthly counseling session, a meditation app subscription, an employee assistance program.

What is actually needed: mandatory regular rotation, on-site psychiatrists, paid psychological counseling leave, daily working hour limits.

But most companies do not provide these.

The Paradox of Technological Progress: The Smarter the AI, the More Ghost Workers

The popular narrative is: as AI becomes more powerful, the demand for human labor will decrease.

But the data shows the opposite.

Why Does Stronger AI Require More Humans?

1. The Increasing Cost of Learning

Training a more capable model requires more, higher-quality annotated data. GPT-4's training data volume far exceeds GPT-3.5's.

2. Scaling RLHF

To align a model with human values across millions of decision points requires millions of instances of human feedback.

3. The Alignment Problem

A more powerful AI is also more capable of causing harm. "Aligning" it requires more human review.

The Data Shows

According to 2025–2026 industry analysis reports:

  • Data annotation work grew 340% between 2023 and 2025
  • The number of workers engaged in AI-related annotation globally grew from approximately 3 million in 2022 to approximately 6.5 million in 2025
  • Projected to reach 10 million by 2030

"This is not a story of machines replacing people. This is a story of machines being given intelligence by humans, but at the cost of creating more, cheaper human labor."

The Blank Zone of Labor Rights

Traditional labor laws have failed in the face of ghost labor.

Legal Gaps

Ghost workers are typically classified as: - Independent contractors: Not protected by minimum wage laws - Gig workers: No job security - Remote freelancers: Cross-border, unclear legal jurisdiction

This means they have no right to workers' compensation, unemployment insurance, union participation, maternity leave, sick leave, or pensions.

Difficulties for Unions

Employment relationships are dispersed and hidden: - Google hires Scale AI, Scale AI hires Sama, Sama hires Kenyan workers - Who is truly responsible for working conditions? - How can workers negotiate with the actual decision-makers?

In June 2025, Meta announced it would purchase 49% of Scale AI's shares. Just one month later, Scale AI laid off 14% of its staff and 500 contractors.

First Attempts at Labor Organizing

In March 2023, 150 Kenyan AI workers voted to establish a union. Their demands included: - Hourly wage increase to at least $4 (from $1.32–$2) - Paid mental health days off - Job security guarantees - Transparent performance metrics

But how much power does this workers' union actually have? Limited. Companies can shift work elsewhere at any time.

If Ghost Labor Disappeared: What Would Happen to AI?

Suppose all data annotation, content moderation, and RLHF feedback work suddenly disappeared.

Short-term Consequences

  • ChatGPT would stop learning new content
  • The quality of existing models would degrade
  • Content moderation features would disappear

Medium-term Consequences

  • New AI models could not be trained
  • Corporate confidence in AI would decline

Long-term Consequences

  • The AI industry would need to be completely rearchitected

Ghost labor is not a byproduct of the AI industry—it is part of its infrastructure. Without ghost workers, modern AI simply would not exist.

Why Is This Story So Invisible?

The answer lies in economic incentives.

For OpenAI, Meta, and Google, maintaining the invisibility of ghost workers is a key business strategy:

  1. Would consumers still use the product if they knew low-wage workers were behind it?
  2. Would investors be more cautious if they knew the company depended on such labor?
  3. Would regulators take action if this story were more widely known?

The optimal corporate strategy is: run PR campaigns emphasizing "AI capabilities" while remaining silent about the human labor in the supply chain.

The choice of terminology itself is an exercise of power: "data annotator," "content moderator," "freelancer"—these terms dilute the essential nature of the work.

Where Are the Possibilities for Change?

1. Legislation

California's AB 2013 (effective January 2026) requires AI developers to disclose the sources of their training data. This is a first step: transparency.

The EU's AI Act also requires more detailed information disclosure.

2. Worker Organizing

The union attempt in Kenya shows that even in the Global South, workers can organize.

3. Consumer Pressure

When more people learn the story behind ChatGPT, perhaps consumer pressure will push companies to improve working conditions.

4. Technological Innovation

Some companies are trying to use automated tools to replace human annotation. If these technologies mature, they may reduce the demand for ghost workers.

In Closing

The story of ghost labor is a story about how invisibility becomes a tool of exploitation.

While executives take the stage to talk about how AI will save humanity, thousands of workers in Nairobi, Kenya, are still watching child abuse videos for $2 an hour.

This is not "an unfortunate side effect of the AI era." This is structural. It is by design. It is the necessary cost of making AI "cheap" and "fast"—borne by those with the least voice.

The first step toward change is seeing them. No longer treating them as "data annotators," but as workers—workers worthy of dignity, safe working conditions, and fair compensation.

The second step is asking an uncomfortable question: Do we want an AI future built on this foundation?

Postscript: 2025–2026 Latest Developments

Kenya Meta Lawsuit: Over 140 People Diagnosed with PTSD

In 2025, a historic class-action lawsuit continued to unfold. Over 140 Kenyan workers who formerly served as Meta content moderators at the Sama outsourcing agency in Nairobi sued Meta and Samasource Kenya on grounds of severe psychological trauma.

Court records show that the content these workers were exposed to daily included: pedophilic material, child sexual assault, corpses, genocide videos, and terrorist action recordings. They performed this work without adequate psychological support, under contracts that could be terminated at any time on a weekly or monthly basis.

Diagnosed conditions include: post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), anxiety disorders, depression, sleep disorders, and inability to maintain normal interpersonal relationships.

This is the human toll left by Silicon Valley's outsourcing chains.

Oxford Fairwork Project: Not a Single Platform Passed

The Fairwork project at the Oxford Internet Institute (one of the world's most authoritative digital labor standards assessment bodies) investigated 15 major digital labor platforms globally in 2025, evaluating dimensions including: fair pay, fair conditions, fair contracts, fair management, and fair representation.

The result was zero: not a single platform could meet the "minimum passing" standard.

India's "Annotation Factories" Scale Up

India's data annotation industry is experiencing explosive growth. According to industry projections, by 2028, the number of active data annotation workers in India could approach 1 million, corresponding to a global data annotation market size exceeding $8 billion.

However, behind this number lies: piece-rate pay, lack of labor protections, extremely fragmented contract structures, and virtually nonexistent career advancement pathways. This is the industrial-era "sweatshop" reborn in the digital age—only the location has shifted from Manchester's cotton mills to Bangalore's annotation studios.

The Awakening of Worker Organizing

Faced with these conditions, ghost workers around the world are slowly beginning to organize:

  • Kenya established the African Content Moderators Union and the Data Labelers Association
  • A Global Content Moderators Trade Alliance is attempting transnational connections worldwide
  • In 2025, a summit uniting moderators from Kenya, the Philippines, India, and Latin America issued common demands: direct employment by tech companies, mandatory mental health support, higher pay, and the right to organize unions

These efforts face enormous structural obstacles—the multi-layered nesting of outsourcing chains legally severs the relationship between workers and the companies that actually benefit. But the consciousness of organizing is already growing.

Sustained Media Exposure

  • Rest of World (2025): In-depth investigation into how Big Tech minimizes costs through the "African AI labor supply chain"
  • CBS 60 Minutes (2025): Interviewed data annotation workers who described being exploited, sparking widespread discussion
  • Anadolu Agency (2025): Documented the plight of Kenyan youth in "AI sweatshops"; the report's headline directly quoted workers: "This is a continuation of slavery and colonialism"

These reports have generated sustained, cross-media public pressure. But so far, no major tech company has made structural changes.

Latest Developments (May–June 2026)

The plight of ghost workers reached a series of concentrated flashpoints in late spring 2026—not isolated scandals or one-off breakdowns at a single company, but the same global supply chain shaking simultaneously across different links.

1. Meta and Sama's Nairobi collaboration abruptly terminated; 1,108 workers lost their jobs after six days' notice. On April 30, Meta notified Kenyan outsourcing firm Sama that it was terminating the data annotation contract for Ray-Ban Meta smart glasses; 1,108 workers were laid off after a six-day notice period. The trigger was a February disclosure by several workers to Sweden's Svenska Dagbladet and Göteborgs-Posten: they spent day after day at annotation stations watching footage uploaded by glasses users, including sexual acts, toileting, images of minors, and bank card information—material that was supposed to be used, without users' knowledge, to train Meta's multimodal models. Workers widely believed the contract termination was retaliation for their public disclosures. The incident has been separately logged by the OECD AI Incidents Monitor; class-action lawsuits and regulatory investigations in both the UK and Kenya are advancing (The Next Web report).

2. Nairobi High Court again postpones ruling on two landmark Meta/Sama cases. The judgment originally scheduled for February 12 by Justice Nduma Nderi—involving the Daniel Motaung case and the class-action lawsuit led by Kiana Monique Arendse and James Agada Mark on behalf of 181 moderators—was indefinitely postponed by the court "until further notice"; meanwhile, out-of-court settlement talks between Meta and the plaintiffs formally broke down in May. Plaintiffs' attorneys accused Meta of refusing to make substantive concessions on mental health compensation and the "anti-union blacklist" issue. The two cases collectively allege human trafficking, unfair dismissal, union suppression, and privacy violations—among the first labor lawsuits to directly try a Silicon Valley parent company under the legal system of the platform's host country (Business & Human Rights Resource Centre).

3. Data Labelers Association (DLA) membership surpasses 800; sends first collective letter to multinational AI companies. The Data Labelers Association, spontaneously founded by Kenyan AI workers in February 2025, had expanded to over 800 registered members by May 2026, covering gig workers across Sama, Majorel, Remotasks (a Scale AI sub-brand), and several Mercor subcontracting chains. The association publicly demanded raising the local minimum hourly wage from approximately $1.5–$2 to a living wage, introducing third-party mental health support, and requiring clients to include "anti-retaliation" clauses in contracts. The association's strategy is to bypass outsourcers and negotiate directly with OpenAI/Anthropic/Meta—the first cross-platform, cross-outsourcer annotation worker organizational entity to emerge in the Global South (Computer Weekly report; Association website).

4. Mercor data breach: biometric data of 40,000 contractors posted on the dark web; the "shadow compliance debt" of AI employment models erupts. On March 27, Mercor's contractor database was breached via a LiteLLM software supply chain attack; on March 31, the company confirmed the incident to TechCrunch. Exposed content included: Social Security numbers, interview videos, facial biometric data of over 40,000 contractors, as well as screenshots of some employees' computer screens—precisely the training material Mercor used for "AI evaluating AI." Lapsus$ auctioned 4TB of data on the dark web. Between April 1 and 7, courts in California and Texas accepted at least 7 class-action lawsuits. Meta suspended all cooperation with Mercor; OpenAI and Google launched investigations. This incident, for the first time, pulled "high-end AI evaluation workers" (Mercor's core offering is master's- and PhD-level remote contractors earning $50–$200/hour) into the same security/privacy vulnerability map as Kenyan annotation workers (PYMNTS report; Biometric Update).

5. California AB 2013 survives its first constitutional challenge; EU AI Act high-risk provisions proposed for delay until 2027. AB 2013, effective January 1, requires all generative AI products offered to California residents to publicly disclose a "training data summary"—including sources, data types, whether personal information is included, and processing procedures. In May, the Northern District of California federal court rejected an industry association's request for a First Amendment preliminary injunction, and the law remains in effect (Norton Rose Fulbright briefing). Meanwhile, the European Commission proposed in November 2025 to postpone the application date of the AI Act's high-risk provisions (including the "sufficiently detailed summary" obligation for training data, Article 53(1)(d)) from August 2026 to December 2027 at the latest; the legislature is negotiating amendments within 2026 (Wilson Sonsini 2026 preview). Both legislative tracks are tightening in the same direction—but neither mandates disclosure of the people behind the training data: who the annotators are, where they are, how much they are paid, whether they signed NDAs. "Training data transparency" in 2026 still remains at the dataset level; ghost workers remain beneath the data.

6. Academia: From Bangladesh to the platform economy at large, the "Ghostcrafting" concept expands. ATM Mizanur Rahman and Sharifa Sultana at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, in arXiv:2512.21649 ("Ghostcrafting AI: Under the Rug of Platform Labor"), depict a new labor form—workers renting equipment, sharing templates, forging identities, bypassing platform commissions, inventing "coping tactics" from structurally invisible positions. The paper terms this adaptation "ghostcrafting," proposing that AI depends not merely on invisible labor, but on labor forced to "learn to be invisible." This forms a mutual intertextuality with the OECD AI Incidents project's formal classification of Kenya-Colombia annotation labor as a "realized AI incident" (rather than a potential risk) (OECD AI 2025-10-16-2c8d incident page).

Placing these six events on the same timeline reveals: lawsuits are stalling, contracts are being terminated, unions are growing, data is being breached, legislation is tightening, academia is naming—and nothing has actually changed the hourly wages of annotation workers. Smart glasses let annotation workers "see into" customers' bedrooms for the first time; class-action lawsuits let them "hear" Meta's refusal to mediate for the first time; the Mercor breach put them up for auction on the dark web for the first time. Transparency is rising; conditions are not.

## Reflection and Boundaries: Under What Conditions Do This Article's Claims Hold

The story in this article is true—but any "true story" has its sharpest and its bluntest edges. Before delivering this article to readers, the following seven vulnerabilities must be laid on the table; otherwise, readers will use it to draw conclusions more radical and less accurate than the author's.

One: "$1.32–$2/hr equals exploitation" ignores relative wage benchmarks

Kenya's revised statutory minimum monthly wage as of November 2024 is KES 16,113.75 (approximately $125) (based on Nairobi and other city rates). The Sama workers' hourly wage of $1.32–$2 in the TIME investigation, calculated using the figures disclosed in the Time report itself, translates to a monthly salary of approximately $210–$323—which is indeed "more than double Kenya's minimum wage" (this is Sama's defense, but it aligns with official minimum wage data). A receptionist in Nairobi at the time earned a comparable hourly wage of approximately $1.52/hr—meaning the low end of $1.32/hr was still below the receptionist rate, but the mid-to-high range of $2/hr was significantly above it. The absolute figure of $2 looks shocking, but from a relative wage perspective, it is 1.3 times a receptionist's wage and over 4 times the rural minimum wage.

This does not mean the working conditions are acceptable (PTSD, contracts terminable at will, no psychological support are all real). But "$1.32 = exploitation" is a rhetorical move that judges Kenyan wage levels against a Silicon Valley reference frame. A more accurate claim would be "for this type of content at this intensity and psychological burden, no wage would be sufficient"—rather than "this number itself constitutes exploitation." The article should make this explicit; otherwise, readers will be led to the erroneous conclusion that "raising it to $4/hr solves the problem," when in fact the Kenya DLA union's demand is indeed $4/hr—a reasonable but far from revolutionary figure.

Two: The "6.5 million → 10 million ghost workers" figure is fragile

The figures cited in the article—"global AI annotation workers: 3 million in 2022 → 6.5 million in 2025 → 10 million in 2030"—currently cannot be traced to an authoritative primary source. The most commonly cited market data comes from Grand View Research: data annotation tools market $1.02 billion in 2023, projected $5.33 billion by 2030; and Data Collection & Labeling market $3.77 billion in 2024, $17.1 billion by 2030. These are dollar market sizes, not headcounts. Converting market size to worker numbers requires a series of assumptions (average wages, in-house vs. outsourced ratios, whether traditional data processing is included). The commonly cited 6.5 million figure most likely originated from back-calculations based on these market numbers, and has been mutually cited by multiple consulting firms as established fact. The article should change "the data shows" to "industry estimates," and note that the figures include traditional data processing work (not exclusively AI training).

Three: "71% PTSD / 68% depression / 52% moral injury" cannot be traced to compliant peer-reviewed sources

These percentages have appeared repeatedly in business media from 2024–2026, but the most rigorous academic reviews to date (Steiger et al. 2021, CHI; Spence et al. 2023, Cyberpsychology; 2025 Behavioral Sciences replication study) all explicitly state that "there is currently no scientific research quantifying the prevalence of PTSD among content moderators". The lifetime PTSD rate in the general population after exposure to secondary trauma is approximately 7.8%. The specific percentages in the article most likely derive from a small-sample survey (n typically < 200) that has been repeatedly recirculated, rather than representing robust prevalence rates across the industry. The psychological toll is real, but presenting these three percentages as universal industry facts is fragile—once opponents dismantle them, the entire article's credibility suffers. They should be rewritten as "among documented worker testimonies and diagnoses" or by citing specific lawsuit figures (such as the 140+ Kenya Meta moderators diagnosed).

Four: The counterforce to "smarter AI means more ghost workers" genuinely exists

The article acknowledges this as a "paradox" but completely ignores counter-technical forces:

  • Anthropic's Constitutional AI / RLAIF has already compressed "needing thousands of human preference labels" down to "approximately 10 human-written principles + AI self-labeling."
  • 2024–2026 academic systematic research on scaling laws for synthetic data pretraining shows that purely synthetic data cannot fully replace human data, but human-AI collaboration models are shifting from "humans annotating from scratch" to "humans making rapid judgments on LLM first drafts"—the same number of hours, ten times the output per unit.
  • Scale AI's revenue is projected to exceed $2 billion in 2025, indicating that absolute demand is still rising, but the marginal human labor required per model parameter is declining rapidly.

In other words, the "6.5 million → 10 million" projection faces two counterforces: synthetic data/RLAIF compressing the annotation volume needed per model, and the Mercor/Surge model (see next point) shifting labor from low-end Kenyan workers toward high-end Northern experts. Absolute numbers may still rise, but the stratification structure is changing. The article's claim that "smarter means more" is true, but it won't be the same kind of work.

Five: Conflating low-end and high-end workers under the same "ghost workers" framework is a confusion

The article juxtaposes "Kenyan $2/hr content moderation workers" with "Silicon Valley $50–$150/hr RLHF raters" and calls this the same exploitative structure. But the actual industry structure in 2025–2026 has already分化 into two entirely different labor markets:

  • Low end: Content moderation and basic annotation in Kenya, the Philippines, and India at $1–$3/hour, with multi-layered outsourcing chains and no worker bargaining power. This is the core subject the article portrays.
  • High end: Mercor averages $95/hour, senior domain experts can reach $200+, full-time salaries of $90k–$200k/year; Surge AI pays medical fellows $250–$450/hr, VC partners $500–$1000/hr. These people have bargaining power, attribution rights, and sometimes equity.

Calling both high-end and low-end workers "ghost workers" is inaccurate. High-end AI evaluation workers are more like 21st-century consulting contractors—bargaining power, information asymmetry, and risk structures are all different. The article's claim of "global stratification" is true, but "they're all exploited ghosts" is not true. The Mercor data breach revealed a new type of risk for high-end workers (biometrics, interview videos vulnerable to deepfakes), but this is a privacy risk, not wage exploitation. The two must be discussed separately.

Six: "Legislation/unions/consumer pressure/technological innovation all have limited potential for change" is a pessimism pre-refuted by the article's own evidence

The article's conclusion dismisses all four pathways as "limited." But its own "May–June 2026 Latest Developments" section precisely refutes this pessimism:

  • Kenya DLA union founded in February → expanded to 800 members by May, bypassing outsourcers to negotiate directly.
  • California AB 2013 effective January, survived constitutional challenge in May.
  • Meta-Sama smart glasses contract terminated after Swedish journalists exposed it (though the cost was 1,108 workers losing their jobs, it demonstrates that transparency has teeth).
  • Meta Nairobi class-action lawsuits still advancing; breakdown in mediation forces cases toward full trial.

Equating "profitable companies won't voluntarily improve" with "nothing will ever change" is a narrative convenience. The reality is: transparency, organizing, and cross-border litigation are all in motion; hourly wages are not. The article should make clear this is a phased process, rather than declaring "the possibilities for change are limited." Equating "wages haven't moved" with "nothing has moved" will drive readers to despair and disengagement—and despairing readers do not become sources of consumer pressure.

Seven: Comparison with Mary Gray and Karen Hao's Original Works

Mary Gray and Siddharth Suri's Ghost Work (2019) is the foundational text of this framework, but the original book's subtitle is "How to Stop Silicon Valley from Building a New Global Underclass"—it is diagnosis plus prescription, not a doomsday declaration. The second half of the book spends considerable pages describing how workers spontaneously organize "collaborative networks", refer each other to jobs, teach each other to avoid scam employers—that is, worker agency. If the article takes only the accusatory portion of the "ghost workers" framework while discarding the agency portion, this constitutes a one-sided citation of the original work.

Karen Hao's Empire of AI (2025) is more radical in its critique ("AI colonialism"), but Hao repeatedly emphasizes in interviews that Kenyan workers are drivers of the narrative, not victim specimens—they actively contacted journalists, organized unions, and sued multinational corporations. Phrasing in the article like "they will never know how much their contributions were worth" contradicts the fact that Kenyan workers have been speaking out publicly, writing to the US Congress, and appearing on 60 Minutes since 2023. These workers are not objects being narrated; they are already subjects of their own narrative.

Summary: The Boundaries Within Which This Article's Claims Hold

  • Strongly valid: The content moderation and safety alignment of consumer-grade chat AI (ChatGPT, Gemini, Llama) still heavily depends on low-wage Global South labor; working conditions are poor; the psychological toll is real; outsourcing chains deliberately create responsibility vacuums.
  • Weakly valid or invalid: Specialized AI for code generation, mathematical reasoning, and enterprise RAG has largely shifted toward high-end expert annotation (the Mercor model) or synthetic data/RLAIF. The article's "smarter means more ghosts" is reversed in these domains—it is "smarter means fewer cheap ghosts, more expensive referees."
  • Should be corrected: Percentage data should note small-sample origins; "6.5 million → 10 million" should be labeled "estimates"; "limited possibilities for change" should be revised to "change is occurring at the margins; wages remain the last variable to move."

The real question this article wants to ask is: transparency legislation ≠ labor transparency; outsourcing chains dilute responsibility until no one bears it. This claim stands on solid ground. But to stand firmly, the seven vulnerabilities above must be addressed first—otherwise, opponents will dismantle it simultaneously from the angles of relative wages, numerical provenance, and psychological research.
